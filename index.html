What is Forging?
Overview, history, classification, and advantages
Core Definition

Forging is a deformation process in which the workpiece is compressed between two dies using either impact or gradual pressure to form the part. It is one of the oldest manufacturing processes — dating back to ~4000–5000 BCE.

★
Exam Tip: Forging produces parts with controlled grain flow, giving superior strength, toughness, and fatigue resistance compared to casting or machining. This is a favourite comparison question.
Key Applications
  • Engine crankshafts & connecting rods
  • Gears, turbine blades
  • Aircraft structural parts
  • Hand tools, cutlery, bolts
  • Landing gear components
  • Ship propeller shafts (up to 23 m!)
Advantages of Forging
  • Favorable grain flow orientation
  • Higher strength & toughness
  • Better than casting for critical parts
  • Conservation of material
  • High production rates (automated)
  • Near net-shape possible (precision)
Temperature Classification
Cold Forging

Performed at room temperature. Requires higher forces, but produces excellent surface finish, dimensional accuracy, and strain hardening.

↑ Strength due to work hardening
Warm Forging

Between room temp and hot forging. Reduces forces while retaining some strain hardening benefits. Al alloys: 200–300°C, Steels: 550–750°C.

Hot Forging

Most common. Above recrystallization temperature. Lower forces needed, better ductility. Poorer surface finish than cold forging.

n = 0 (perfectly plastic) in hot forging, so Yf = Y (yield strength)
Load Application: Hammers vs Presses
Forging Hammer

Applies impact load (sudden). Also called drop hammer. Energy limited. Operates at high speed — reduces cooling of hot forging.

Forging Press

Applies gradual pressure. Includes mechanical, hydraulic, and screw presses. Better for precision work.

Types of Forging
Three main classifications by die constraint + precision forging
Open-Die Forging (Upset Forging)

Work is compressed between two flat (or nearly flat) dies. Metal flows without lateral constraint — free to spread outward. Simplest type.

Open-die upsetting — height decreases, diameter increases, barreling due to friction

Barreling Effect

Friction at die-workpiece interface resists outward flow at surface, causing the barrel shape. Worse in hot forging with cold dies (thermal gradient + higher μ).

Related Operations

  • Fullering – convex dies, redistribute metal
  • Edging – concave dies, gather metal
  • Cogging – sequential compression along length
★
Exam Tip: Open-die forgings have simple shapes (shafts, disks, rings). They create favorable grain structure but require more machining. Key benefit: any size range, from pins to 275-metric-ton forgings.
Impression-Die Forging (Closed-Die Forging)

Dies contain the inverse shape of the desired part. Work takes the shape of the die cavity. A portion of metal flows into the gap forming flash.

Impression-die forging sequence — flash forms in the gap between die plates
Role of Flash — Critical Concept!
1

Flash begins to form in the gap

Metal flows outward toward the parting line

2

Friction resists further flow into gap

High pressure + friction hill effect constrains bulk material to remain in die cavity

3

In hot forging: flash cools rapidly

Thin flash against cold dies increases resistance to deformation → further pressure buildup

4

Result: Die cavity filled completely

High pressure forces material into intricate details → high-quality product

★
Exam Favourite: Flash serves a FUNCTIONAL purpose — it restricts metal outflow and increases internal pressure so the die fills completely. It is NOT simply waste. Flash is later trimmed off in a trimming operation.
Flashless (True Closed-Die) Forging

Workpiece is completely contained within the die cavity. No flash forms. Volume of blank must precisely match die cavity volume.

Requirements

  • Very precise blank volume control
  • Simple, symmetrical part geometries
  • Best for Al, Mg alloys
  • Often classified as precision forging

Risks

  • Blank too large → die/press damage
  • Blank too small → incomplete cavity fill
Special Case: Coining is a flashless forging — fine surface details, Kf = 6
💡
Memory Aid: Think "NO flash → NO forgiveness." Flashless forging has zero tolerance for volume errors — if volume is off, disaster strikes either the die or the part quality.
Precision Forging (Net-Shape / Near-Net-Shape)

Advanced impression-die forging producing thinner sections, closer tolerances, and minimal machining allowance. May still have small flash.

Force & Strain Analysis
All key formulas with worked examples — the most exam-tested section
1. True Strain in Open-Die Forging
True Strain
ε = ln(h₀ / h)
h₀ = initial height (mm or in)
h = current height at any point in process
Maximum strain reached when h = hf (final height)
★
Note: In hot forging, n = 0 (perfectly plastic), so Yf = Y = constant. In cold forging, Yf = Kεⁿ increases with strain.
2. Basic Upsetting Force (Ideal — No Friction)
Force — Ideal Conditions
F = Yf × A
F = forging force (N or lb)
Yf = flow stress at current strain = Kεⁿ (MPa)
A = cross-sectional area at current height (mm²)

Note: A = V/h (from constant volume), where V = initial volume
3. Force with Friction — Shape Factor (Most Important!)
Upsetting Force with Friction (Open-Die)
F = Kf × Yf × A
Kf = 1 + (0.4μD/h)
Kf = forging shape factor (accounts for friction and D/h ratio)
μ = coefficient of friction between work and die
D = workpart diameter (or contact length dimension) mm
h = current workpart height mm
Yf = flow stress MPa  |  A = cross-sectional area mm²
★
Key Insight: As h decreases and D increases, Kf increases, meaning the forging force increases sharply near the end of the stroke — even beyond what Yf×A alone predicts. Maximum force is always at the END of the stroke.
4. Impression-Die Forging Force
Impression/Flashless Force
F = Kf × Yf × A
SAME formula — different interpretation:
A = projected area of the part INCLUDING flash (mm²)
Kf = shape factor from table (not the friction equation)
Yf = flow stress at forging temperature
Part ShapeKf Value
Simple shapes, without flash (flashless)3–5
Simple shapes, with flash5–8 (or 6.0)
Complex shapes, with flash8–12 (or 8.0)
Very complex shapes with flash10.0
Coining (flashless, top & bottom)6.0
5. Friction Hill (Pressure Distribution)
Die Pressure at any radius r
p = Y × e^(2μ(r₀ - r)/h)
p = die pressure at radius r
Y = flow stress of material
r₀ = outer radius of workpiece
r = radius at point of interest
At r = r₀ (outer edge): p = Y (minimum)
At r = 0 (center): p = maximum (highest pressure at center)
💡
Friction Hill: Pressure distribution is NOT uniform — it peaks at the center and decreases toward the outer edge. This bell-shaped curve is called the "friction hill."
6. Hubbing Force
Hubbing Force
F = 3 × UTS × A
UTS = ultimate tensile strength of material (MPa)
A = projected area of the impression (mm²)
Worked Example — Open-Die Forging
Example Problem (from Textbook)

A cylindrical workpiece is cold upset forged. Starting: h₀ = 75 mm, D₀ = 50 mm. Reduced to hf = 36 mm. Flow curve: K = 350 MPa, n = 0.17. μ = 0.1. Find force at h = 62 mm.

1

Find volume (constant throughout)

V = π × (50²/4) × 75 = 147,262 mm³

2

Calculate true strain at h = 62 mm

ε = ln(75/62) = ln(1.21) = 0.190

3

Calculate flow stress Yf

Yf = Kεⁿ = 350 × (0.190)^0.17 = 264 MPa

4

Find new area and diameter (constant volume)

A = V/h = 147,262/62 = 2,375 mm²; D = √(4A/π) = 55.0 mm

5

Calculate Kf

Kf = 1 + (0.4 × 0.1 × 55)/62 = 1 + 0.0355 = 1.035

6

Calculate forging force

F = 1.035 × 264 × 2,375 = 649,303 N ≈ 649 kN

★
Pattern to remember: Force at h = 36 mm (final) was 1,467,422 N. Force increased from ~245 kN to ~1,467 kN as the workpiece was compressed — nearly 6× increase!
Other Forging Operations
Heading, piercing, cogging, swaging, hubbing, orbital, isothermal, coining
Heading (Upset Forging)

Upsetting operation on the end of a rod/wire to increase cross-section. Makes nails, bolt heads, rivets, fasteners.

Key rule: Unsupported length-to-diameter ratio must be < 3:1 to prevent buckling

High production rates (hundreds/min for small parts). Machines: Headers (automated) or horizontal upsetters for large parts.

Piercing

Indenting (not breaking through) the surface with a punch to produce a cavity. Example: hexagonal cavity in bolt heads.

Piercing pressure = 3–5 × material strength

May be followed by punching to create a through-hole.

Cogging (Drawing Out)

Sequence of forging compressions along the length of a workpiece using open dies with flat/slightly contoured surfaces. Reduces cross-section, increases length.

Used in steel industry to produce blooms and slabs from cast ingots. Also called incremental forging.

Coining

Special closed-die forging impressing fine details into top and bottom surfaces. Little metal flow, but very high pressures (5–6× material strength).

No lubricant! Lubricant gets trapped and prevents full detail reproduction

Kf = 6. Used for coins, medallions, gears, medical devices. Also called sizing when used to improve dimensional accuracy.

Rotary Swaging

Also called radial forging. Rod or tube subjected to radial impact forces by reciprocating dies. Up to 20 strokes/second.

  • Max workpiece diameter: ~150 mm
  • Tolerances: ±0.05 to ±0.5 mm
  • Makes screwdriver blades, soldering tips, gun barrel rifling
  • Also used for pointing and sizing
Hubbing

Pressing a hardened punch into a metal block to create a die cavity used for tableware, small parts.

F = 3 × UTS × A
A = projected area of impression
Advanced Forging Operations
Orbital Forging

Upper die moves in orbital path (like mortar/pestle). Forms part incrementally — low forces because contact is on small area at any instant. 10–20 cycles per part. Good for disk-shaped, conical parts (bevel gears).

Isothermal Forging (Hot-Die Forging)

Dies heated to same temperature as workpiece. No heat loss → low forging load, better material flow. Complex shapes in one press stroke. Dies: nickel or molybdenum alloys. Expensive, low production rate — but ideal for Ti, Al, superalloys.

★
Exam Tip — Swaging vs Cogging: Both involve progressive deformation. Swaging uses radial reciprocating forces on round bar/tube sections. Cogging uses sequential linear compressions along the length of a bar with open flat dies. Very different mechanisms!
Forging Equipment
Hammers vs presses — characteristics, limitations, speed ranges
Forging Hammers — Impact Loading

Apply sudden impact loads. Energy limited. High speed minimizes cooling of hot forging. Most versatile, least expensive type.

Gravity Drop

  • Energy = weight × drop height
  • Ram: 180–4,500 kg
  • Energy: up to 120 kJ
  • Speed: 3.6–4.8 m/s

Power Drop

  • Ram accelerated by steam/air/hydraulic
  • Ram: 225–22,500 kg
  • Energy: up to 1,150 kJ
  • Speed: 3.0–9.0 m/s

Counterblow

  • Two rams approach simultaneously
  • Less vibration to foundation
  • Capacity: up to 1,200 kJ
  • Speed: 4.5–9.0 m/s
★
Disadvantage of drop hammers: Large amount of impact energy transmitted through anvil into building floor. Vibration problem.
Forging Presses — Gradual Pressure
Press TypeDriveLimitation TypeSpeed (m/s)Best For
Mechanical (crank/eccentric) Flywheel + crank Stroke limited 0.06–1.5 High precision, high production
Hydraulic Hydraulic piston Load limited 0.06–0.30 Large, complex parts; long stroke
Screw Flywheel + screw Energy limited 0.6–1.2 Thin parts, turbine blades

Mechanical Press Details
  • Stroke limited — speed = 0 at bottom
  • Very high force at end of stroke
  • Capacity: 2.7 to 107 MN
  • Best for precision parts
  • Higher prod. rate, easier to automate
Hydraulic Press Details
  • Load limited — stops if capacity exceeded
  • Constant force through full stroke
  • Capacity: up to 730 MN (closed-die)
  • Higher initial cost, less maintenance
  • Boeing 747 landing gear forged in 450-MN press!
💡
Memory Aid — Press Limitations: "Strokes (Screw & Mechanical)" = Energy & Stroke limited. Hydraulic = Load limited (it just stops). Hammers = Energy limited. Key exam question: Which presses are suited for long strokes? → Screw and hydraulic (constant force throughout).
Die Design Terminology
Key features, principles, materials, and lubrication
Impression die terminology — parting line, flash land, gutter, draft angle, fillet, corner, rib, web
Parting Line

Plane dividing upper from lower die. Called the flash line in impression-die forging. Selection affects grain flow, required load, and flash formation. Usually at the largest cross-section.

Draft Angle

Taper on sides of die cavity needed to remove part from die. Part shrinks on cooling → internal angles larger than external.

Al & Mg parts: 3°
Steel parts: 5°–7°
Internal draft: 7°–10° (larger than external 3°–5°)
Precision forgings: ≈ 0°
Flash Design Guidelines
  • Flash thickness ≈ 3% of max forging thickness
  • Land length = 2–5× flash thickness
  • Excess flash flows into gutter to avoid load buildup
Radii — Fillets & Corners

Small radii = limited metal flow + higher die surface stresses + fatigue cracking risk. Make radii as large as design allows. Small fillet radii also cause rapid die wear from stress concentration.

Webs and Ribs
  • Web: thin portion parallel to parting line
  • Rib: thin portion perpendicular to parting line
  • Both cause difficulty as they become thinner
Die Materials Requirements
  • Strength & toughness at elevated temp
  • Hardenability, uniform hardening
  • Resistance to thermal/mechanical shock
  • Wear resistance (scale in hot forging)
  • Common: tool/die steels with Cr, Ni, Mo, V
Die Failure Causes
● Improper die design
● Wrong die material
● Bad heat treatment
● Overheating / heat checking
● Excessive die wear
● Overloading
● Misalignment of die components
● Misuse / improper handling
Lubrication Functions
  • Reduces friction → lower forging forces
  • Reduces die wear → longer die life
  • Controls metal flow into die cavity
  • Acts as thermal barrier (hot workpiece ↔ cold dies)
  • Acts as parting agent (prevents sticking)
  • Exception: Coining uses NO lubricant
Forgeability & Forging Defects
Material forgeability tests, common defects, and how to prevent them
Forgeability Definition

Forgeability = capability of material to undergo deformation in forging without cracking. Rated on: ductility & strength, forging temperature required, frictional behavior, quality of forgings produced.

Test 1: Upsetting Test

Solid cylinder upset between flat dies. Greater deformation before surface cracks appear = higher forgeability. Simple, widely accepted.

Test 2: Hot-Twist Test

Round specimen twisted continuously until failure at different temperatures. Temperature where maximum twists before failure = optimal forging temperature. Especially useful for steels.

Common Forging Defects
Laps (Due to Web Buckling)
1

Insufficient blank volume

Not enough material to fill die cavity

2

Web buckles during forging

Thin web region folds over itself

3

Laps form in finished forging

Structural weakness, stress raisers

Fix: Increase web thickness
Internal Cracks (Oversized Billet)
1

Oversized blank volume

Excess material in the die

2

Die cavities fill prematurely

Excess material has nowhere to go

3

Material flows past filled regions

Internal cracks develop in ribs

Fix: Precise blank volume control
Other Defect Sources
  • Surface cracking (barreling, hot/cold)
  • Nonuniform deformation in die cavity
  • Temperature gradients in workpiece
  • Microstructural changes (phase transformations)
  • End grains: grain flow reaches surface perpendicularly → stress raisers
  • Die misalignment during forging
★
Key Exam Point: Forging defects can cause fatigue failures, corrosion, and wear in service. Inspection before placing parts in service is critical — especially in aerospace applications. Computer simulation (e.g., DEFORM software using FEM) is now used to predict and avoid defects in die design.

Flow Stress
Yf = K × εⁿ

F = Yf × A

F = Kf × Yf × A

Impression-Die / Flashless Force
F = Kf × Yf × A
A includes flash area; Kf from table (6, 8, or 10)
Forging Force (Kalpakjian Form)
F = Yf × πr² × (1 + 2μr/3h)
r = instantaneous radius, h = height, μ = friction
Friction Hill (Die Pressure)
p = Y × e^(2μ(r₀−r)/h)

UTS = ultimate tensile strength, A = projected area of impression
